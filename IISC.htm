Introduction

As part of my B.Tech final year, I had the privilege of working as a Research Intern at IISc under the guidance of Prof T.V. Sreenivas. Although the internship was officially for one semester (six months), I continued the project for nearly a year to achieve conclusive results in this research endeavor. This opportunity was invaluable as it was my first research-based experience, allowing me to delve deeply into the field of audio signal processing and develop a strong foundation in research methodologies.

Initially, I assisted a Master's student, Omkar Sawant, on a project titled 'Enhanced Directional Sensitivity using Acoustic Dish Reflector', which has since been published as a paper. Due to my contributions and commitment, I was later entrusted with an independent project that focused on a rotating parabolic reflector and its acoustic significance, particularly examining how speed and distance affect its performance. I also developed an algorithm for passive signal localization, which avoided the need for a complex microphone array setup.

Since this research is publicly available, you can explore the project's details in the published paper, which contains comprehensive information about the findings and methodologies.

Internship Experience

During the internship, I initially worked on labeling audio files using Audacity software and a Python script to organize the labels. I then transitioned to the hardware setup, focusing on the mechanical construction and rotation of the parabolic reflector. This involved calibrating the system for a flat frequency response and analyzing the frequency response of the coupled microphone and speaker, as well as understanding how the parabolic reflector influenced these responses.

Further analysis examined the structural impact and material properties of the reflector. In subsequent stages, I addressed motor noise reduction and developed algorithms for sound classification in an open acoustic environment. The setup was based on a server-driven system using Raspberry Pi, motor actuators, and extensive data collection over several months, including overnight sessions. Managing large datasets became a key challenge.

I also worked on both manual and automated data cleaning for the audio files. Additionally, I developed software algorithms for classifying background and event noise, experimenting with models such as Hidden Markov Models (HMM), Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM), and Universal Background Models (UBM). This led to the creation of a sound classification and localization algorithm.

Details of this work were later published under the supervision of Dr. Anirban Bhowmick, who was a postdoc at the time under Prof T.V. Sreenivas.

My Learning Experience

I gained significant insights from the research setup and learned about the workings of research and how to initiate a project similar to this one. Technically, I developed my skills in both the hardware aspects of embedded systems and server setup, including integrating a Raspberry Pi and using a stepper motor for precise angle adjustments. I also learned about selecting drivers for smooth transitions and discovering methods to eliminate audio channel noise in the system.

This experience involved many experimental aspects, such as mechanical setup, readings, and calibration. Alongside this, I deepened my understanding of signal processing, particularly in audio signal processing.
